Steel Structures Design

There are many types of steel structures. Steel design, or more specifically, structural steel design, is an area of structural engineering used to design steel structures and buildings with steel frames. These structures include school buildings, sports gymnasiums, agricultural sheds, housing, commercial office & warehouse buildings, warehouses, commercial retail buildings and aircraft hangers, ships and stadiums.

The design and use of steel frames are commonly employed in the design of steel structures. More advanced structures include steel plates and shells.

In structural engineering, a steel structure is a combination of steel columns, steel trusses (beams) and supporting beams, designed for supporting loads and resisting moments. The effects of loads and moments on steel structures are determined through structural analysis.

A steel structure is composed of structural members that are made of steel, usually with standard cross-sectional profiles and standards of chemical composition and mechanical properties.

There are currently two common methods of structural steel design: The first method is the Allowable Strength Design (ASD) method. The second is the Load and Resistance Factor Design (LRFD) method. Both use a strength, or ultimate level design approach

Steel Structure Design & Fabrication Considerations

Properties of structural steel. The following properties of structural steel are considered before using structural steel columns for building construction. Engineers consider these properties for determining the quality of steel for the project. High quality steel is used so that dependable and long lasting construction is possible. The most important components include the following:

  • Density - The density of the steel is defined as mass per unit volume. Structural steel has a density between 7.75 and 8.1 g/cm3.
  • Elastic Modulus - The modulus of elasticity is the measurement of the tendency of an a steel column, steel beam or supporting brace to be deformed when a force or stress is applied to it. Typical values for structural steel components range from 190-210 gigapascals.
  • Poisson's Ratio - It is the ratio between contraction and elongation of the steel. The lower the value, the less the steel will shrink in thickness when stretched. Acceptable Poisson's Ratio values for structural steel are from 0.27 to 0.3.
  • Tensile Strength - The tensile strength of the steel is the limit where the steel can be stretched without breaking. The fracture point is the point at which the steel breaks after application of stress. Structural steel has a high tensile strength, as such, it is preferred over other materials for building construction.
  • Yield Strength - The yield point is the stress at which the steel deforms permanently and doesn't return to its original shape when stress is removed.  Structural steel, made with alloys, have a yield strength from 366 to 1793 megapascals. Structural steel, made with carbon, have a yield strength from 187 to 758 megapascals.
  • Specific Heat - The specific heat or heat capacity, is the amount of heat which needs to be applied to the steel to raise its temperature by a given amount. A higher value denotes a greater insulation ability. Structural steel made of carbon, has a heat capacity range from 450 to 2081 Joules per Kilogram Kelvin and structural steel made from alloys, have a heat capacity range of 452 to 1499 Joules per Kilogram Kelvin.
  • Hardness - Hardness is the resistance of the steel to shape change when a force is applied to it. There are 3 types of hardness measurements. Scratch, indentation and rebound. Structural steel, made using alloys, has a hardness ranging between 149-627 Kg. Structural steel made using carbon have a hardness range of 86 to 388 Kg.

Erecting Steel Structures

Steel structure erection and construction essentially consists of four main tasks:

  1. SETTING THE BASES - The foundations are marked out and pegged. Then the bases are dug and shuttered if needed, ready for setting the bolts and pouring the concrete. Before commencing with the erecting of the steel structure, the foundation bases are first checked if they are suitable and safe for erection to commence.
  2. LIFTING AND PLACING THE STEEL COMPONENTS - The steel columns and rafters are then lifted into place, using cranes or by jacking. The columns and rafters are then bolted together, but not yet be fully tightened. Bracings are also set and bolted.
  3. ALIGNING THE STRUCTURE - We then check that column bases are lined and level and that the columns are plumb. At this stage we will make any adjustments to ensure the structure is correctly aligned.
  4. BOLTING UP - We then tighten all the bolted connections to secure and impart rigidity to the frame.
  5. SHEETING - We then sheet the roof structure as per the design specifications. Any cladding, barge boards, ridge caps and other finishings are then added to complete the project.
